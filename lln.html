Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers says that as a sample size grows, the sample average gets closer and closer to the true population average.

μ

The Big Idea

Random results can look messy in the short run, but over many independent trials, their average tends to stabilize near the expected value.

x̄

Sample Average

The sample average is what you observe from collected data. The LLN explains why larger samples usually give more reliable estimates.

sample average → expected value
∞

As Samples Grow

With enough observations, early unusual outcomes become less important compared with the total number of trials.

Weak Law of Large Numbers

The sample average converges in probability to the expected value. In practical terms, the chance of a large error becomes very small as the sample size increases.

P(|x̄ₙ − μ| > ε) → 0
S

Strong Law of Large Numbers

The sample average converges almost surely to the expected value. This means convergence happens with probability 1 as the number of observations goes to infinity.

x̄ₙ → μ almost surely
i.i.d.

Independence Matters

The classic LLN assumes observations are independent and identically distributed. One coin flip should not influence the next.

!

Not the Law of Compensation

The LLN does not mean that short-term results must immediately balance out. Getting several heads in a row does not make tails “due” on the next flip.

🎲

What Actually Happens

Early deviations become less important because they are diluted by many later observations. The average stabilizes, but the process does not remember previous outcomes.

50%

Fair Coin Reminder

Every flip of a fair coin still has a 50% chance of heads and a 50% chance of tails, regardless of the previous sequence.

1

Insurance

Insurers cannot predict exactly what will happen to one person, but with millions of policyholders they can estimate total claims more reliably.

2

Casinos

Casinos rely on small mathematical advantages. Individual players may win in the short term, but many repeated bets make long-term results approach the house edge.

3

Polling

Researchers use random samples to estimate what a larger population thinks. Larger, well-designed samples tend to produce more stable estimates.
