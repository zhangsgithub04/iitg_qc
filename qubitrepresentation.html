Representing Qubits
from vectors to spheres

A qubit is the quantum analogue of a bit. Instead of only being 0 or 1, a qubit is represented by a normalized vector in a two-dimensional complex vector space. Dirac notation gives us a compact language, while the Bloch sphere gives us a geometric intuition.

State vectors Dirac notation Bloch sphere Interactive demo Summary

1. Qubits as state vectors

A single qubit state is written as a linear combination of two basis states: |0⟩ and |1⟩. The coefficients are complex numbers called amplitudes.

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩    where    |α|² + |β|² = 1

The normalization condition means the probabilities add to 1. When measured in the computational basis, the probability of seeing 0 is |α|², and the probability of seeing 1 is |β|².

Key idea: Amplitudes can be negative or complex. Probabilities are always real, nonnegative numbers obtained from squared magnitudes.

A vector stores both magnitude and phase information.

2. Dirac notation: kets, bras, and inner products

Ket

|ψ⟩

A ket represents a quantum state vector. For a qubit, it can be viewed as a two-entry column vector.

|ψ⟩ = [ α ; β ]
Bra

⟨ψ|

A bra is the conjugate transpose of a ket. It turns the column vector into a row vector and conjugates complex entries.

⟨ψ| = [ α*   β* ]
Inner product

⟨φ|ψ⟩

The inner product measures overlap between states. Orthogonal states have zero overlap.

⟨0|1⟩ = 0,   ⟨0|0⟩ = 1
Notation shortcut: Dirac notation is especially useful because the same symbols work for simple qubits, multi-qubit systems, gates, measurements, and quantum algorithms.

3. Bloch sphere intuition

Every pure single-qubit state can be represented as a point on the surface of a sphere. The north pole is |0⟩, the south pole is |1⟩, and superposition states live elsewhere on the surface.

|ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + eiφ sin(θ/2)|1⟩

The angle θ controls how much probability lies in |0⟩ versus |1⟩. The angle φ captures relative phase, which is invisible in one measurement basis but crucial for interference.

  • North pole: |0⟩
  • South pole: |1⟩
  • Equator: equal probabilities
  • Azimuth: relative phase

Measurement reminder

Measurement does not reveal the amplitudes directly. It produces a classical outcome, such as 0 or 1, according to the probabilities determined by the state.

Phase reminder

Two states with the same measurement probabilities can behave differently in a circuit because relative phase affects interference.
