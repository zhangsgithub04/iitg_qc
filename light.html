1. What is phase?

Phase tells us where an oscillation is in its cycle. For light, the electric field oscillates. Two light waves can have the same color and brightness but different phase.

Wave form

E(x,t) = A cos(kx - omega t + phi)

A is amplitude, k describes spatial frequency, omega describes time frequency, and phi is phase offset.

A phase shift changes where peaks and troughs appear. It does not necessarily change amplitude.

Key phase shifts

Phase differenceMeaning
0Waves line up peak with peak.
pi/2Quarter-cycle shift.
piHalf-cycle shift: peak lines up with trough.
2piFull-cycle shift: back to same phase.

2. Why complex numbers enter light waves

Light phase is naturally circular. Complex numbers are ideal for circular motion because e^(i theta) represents rotation in the complex plane.

Compact notation

E(x,t) = Re{ A e^(i(kx - omega t + phi)) }

The physical electric field is real. The complex expression is a compact mathematical tool that stores amplitude and phase together.

Euler's formula

e^(i theta) = cos(theta) + i sin(theta)

Instead of separately tracking sine and cosine, one rotating complex number keeps both. Phase becomes an angle.

Phasor view: phase as a rotating arrow

The phasor arrow stores two things:

Arrow length = amplitudeArrow angle = phaseProjection = real wave value

When light travels, phase changes. Complex notation treats that changing phase as rotation.

3. Phase controls interference

When two light waves meet, their fields add. Phase determines whether they reinforce or cancel.

Constructive interference

e^(i theta) + e^(i theta) = 2e^(i theta)

Same phase: arrows point the same way. The resulting amplitude is larger.

Destructive interference

e^(i theta) + e^(i(theta + pi)) = 0

Opposite phase: arrows point opposite ways. The waves cancel.

In classical light, field amplitudes add first; intensity comes from the squared size of the result. In quantum mechanics, probability amplitudes add first; probabilities come from squared magnitudes.

4. From light waves to photons

Classical light is described as an electromagnetic wave. Quantum light is described in terms of photons and probability amplitudes. Phase still matters.

Classical picture

A light beam has an electric field that oscillates. Phase tells us where the field is in its cycle. Interference appears as bright and dark regions.

intensity proportional to |field amplitude|^2

Quantum picture

A photon does not behave like a tiny classical ball following one visible path. Quantum theory assigns complex amplitudes to possible outcomes.

probability = |complex amplitude|^2
The same mathematical pattern appears in both worlds: add amplitudes first, then square the magnitude to get observable intensity or probability.

5. Light phase as a bridge to quantum phase

Quantum states use complex amplitudes. The phase of these amplitudes can be invisible in one measurement but crucial after interference.

Qubit analogy

|psi> = alpha|0> + beta|1>

The amplitudes alpha and beta may be complex. Their magnitudes affect measurement probabilities, while their relative phase affects interference.

Relative phase matters

|+> = (|0> + |1>)/sqrt(2)
|-> = (|0> - |1>)/sqrt(2)

Both states give 50% 0 and 50% 1 if measured directly. But a later gate can reveal their phase difference.

Quantum phase reveal

No phase flip

|0> -> H -> |+> -> H -> |0>

The final measurement is definitely 0.

With phase flip

|0> -> H -> |+> -> Z -> |-> -> H -> |1>

The hidden phase flip becomes a visible output difference.

Teaching warning: phase is not just a decorative angle. In quantum computing, phase is one of the main things algorithms manipulate so wrong answers cancel and useful answers survive.

6. Beam splitter intuition

A beam splitter gives a photon multiple possible paths. The paths have complex amplitudes. When paths recombine, their phases determine which detector is likely to click.

Path amplitudes

|psi> = a|upper path> + b|lower path>

Changing path length changes the phase of one amplitude. That changes interference when the paths meet again.

Why phase changes detection

If two path amplitudes arrive in phase at one detector, they reinforce there. If they arrive out of phase, they cancel there.

amplitudes interfere before probabilities are calculated
