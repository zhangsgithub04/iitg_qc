Algorithms and Circuits Made from Quantum Gates

A quantum algorithm is built by arranging gates into a circuit. Gates change amplitudes and phases; circuits organize those changes so measurement is more likely to reveal the answer.

1. From gates to circuits to algorithms

A single gate is one operation. A circuit is a sequence of gates. An algorithm is a circuit design that solves a problem by using superposition, phase, interference, entanglement, and measurement.

Important distinction: a quantum algorithm usually does not “try all answers and read them all.” It shapes amplitudes so wrong answers cancel and useful answers become more likely.

2. How to read a circuit diagram

Wires are qubits

Each horizontal wire represents one qubit. Time flows from left to right. A gate placed on a wire changes that qubit.

q0 H Z X M

Columns are moments

Gates in the same vertical layer can often be viewed as happening at the same time. Gates farther right happen later.

|input⟩ → gate 1 → gate 2 → gate 3 → measurement

Mathematically, the total operation is a product of matrices. The rightmost operation acts first when written as a matrix product.

3. Circuit example: make a superposition

The Hadamard gate is often the first step in algorithms because it spreads one definite input into a balanced superposition.

Circuit

|0⟩ H M

State evolution

|0⟩ → H|0⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2

If measured immediately, the outcome is 50% 0 and 50% 1. But in algorithms, we often do more gates before measuring.

4. Circuit example: create entanglement

A common two-qubit pattern is H followed by CNOT. This creates a Bell state.

Bell-state circuit

q0 |0⟩ H M
q1 |0⟩ M

State evolution

|00⟩
→ after H on q0: (|00⟩ + |10⟩)/√2
→ after CNOT: (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2

Measurement gives 00 or 11. The two qubits are correlated, even though each individual qubit looks random.

This is the first major “algorithmic building block”: prepare superposition with H, then use controlled gates like CNOT to connect qubits.

5. What makes a quantum algorithm different?

Superposition

Gates such as H allow amplitudes for many basis states to exist in one state vector.

Phase

Gates such as Z, S, and T change signs or complex phases. These changes may be invisible at first.

Interference

Later gates convert phase differences into probability differences. Useful answers are amplified; bad paths cancel.

Algorithm pattern

prepare → encode problem → interfere → measure

Many quantum algorithms follow this structure. The “problem” is encoded by gates, often called an oracle or subroutine. Interference then turns hidden structure into measurable outcomes.

6. Mini algorithm idea: phase kickback

Phase kickback is a core trick: a controlled operation can place information into phase, and then a Hadamard can convert that phase into a measurement difference.

Simple phase experiment

|0⟩ H Z H M

Why it matters

|0⟩ → H → |+⟩
|+⟩ → Z → |−⟩
|−⟩ → H → |1⟩

The middle Z gate changed phase, not immediate measurement probability. The final H converts that hidden phase difference into a visible output.

7. Example algorithm structure: Deutsch-style question

A classic beginner algorithm asks whether a hidden one-bit function is constant or balanced. The exact details can be taught later; the important circuit idea is the structure.

Circuit skeleton

q0 H H M
q1 X

The middle controlled block represents the hidden function, often called an oracle.

Teaching interpretation

Prepare: set up qubits in useful starting states.
Spread: use H gates to create superposition.
Query: use an oracle to encode the problem into phase or correlation.
Interfere: use more gates so different paths add or cancel.
Measure: read a classical bit string.
Students often ask: “Where is the speedup?” The answer is not just parallelism. The power comes from using phase and interference to extract global structure.

9. How larger algorithms are assembled

Layer What it means Example gates
Initialization Choose starting qubits, usually |0⟩ states. Reset, prepare |0⟩, sometimes X
Superposition Spread amplitude over possible basis states. H on many qubits
Problem encoding Represent the problem as gates, often with controlled operations. CNOT, controlled-Z, oracles
Phase manipulation Mark useful or important states by changing phase. Z, S, T, controlled phase
Interference Convert hidden phase patterns into measurable probability patterns. H, inverse transforms, diffusion steps
Measurement Convert the quantum state into classical bits. M

10. Suggested teaching sequence

Beginner

Teach X, H, Z, measurement, and simple one-wire circuits.

Intermediate

Add CNOT, Bell states, entanglement, and phase-to-probability conversion.

Algorithmic

Teach the pattern: prepare, encode, interfere, measure. Then introduce Deutsch, Grover, or QFT.
